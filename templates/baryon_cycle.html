The Baryon Cycle

A rotational structure in π-algebra

Overview

The baryon mass formulas reveal a cyclic structure that follows the decay path from Omega (sss) to nucleons (uud/udd). This cycle exhibits symmetries in:

  1. The polynomial structure (powers of π)
  2. Charge progression
  3. Magnetic moment family transitions
  4. Lorentz correction cross-references

The cycle can be traversed forward (decay direction: heavy → light) or backward (strangeness accumulation: light → heavy).

Physical Interpretation

The Decay Path

The cycle follows the weak decay chain:

\[ \Omega^- \to \Xi^0/\Xi^- \to \Lambda/\Sigma \to N + \text{pions/kaons} \]

Each step loses one strange quark, descends one \(\pi^5\) level, and splits structure between decuplet and octet.

Angular Structure

The mass coefficient \(c_5\) maps to an angle:

\[ \theta = \frac{c_5 \pi}{5} \]

The proton (c₅ = 6) has angle 216°. Adding strange quarks increases the angle toward 360°. A "complete" baryon would have \(c_5 = 10\), representing a full rotation.

Why the Cross-Reference?

The proton and omega are the dual endpoints of the light baryon cycle. The proton has minimum strangeness (S=0); the omega has maximum strangeness (S=-3). Remarkably, both are relatively stable: the proton is absolutely stable, and the omega lives ~10⁻¹⁰ seconds (weak decay only, since no lighter S=-3 state exists). Their Lorentz corrections reference each other because the cycle is not linear but circular — what leaves at one end returns at the other.
