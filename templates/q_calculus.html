The π-Integer

The fundamental object in q-calculus is the q-integer. With \(q = \pi\):

\[ [n]_\pi = \frac{\pi^n - 1}{\pi - 1} = 1 + \pi + \pi^2 + \cdots + \pi^{n-1} \]

The Grand Unified Formula

All particle coefficients can be expressed as:

\[ \text{coefficient} = \text{quantize}\big( [n]_\pi + k(\pi-1) \big) \]

where:

  • \([n]_\pi = (\pi^n - 1)/(\pi - 1)\) is the π-integer
  • \(k \in \mathbb{Z}\) is an integer shift
  • quantize \(\in \{\lfloor \cdot \rfloor, \lceil \cdot \rceil\}\)

The Fundamental Identity

\[ [2]_\pi + (\pi - 1) = 2\pi \quad \text{exactly!} \]

Proof:

\[ [2]_\pi = \frac{\pi^2 - 1}{\pi - 1} = \frac{(\pi-1)(\pi+1)}{\pi-1} = \pi + 1 \] \[ [2]_\pi + (\pi-1) = (\pi + 1) + (\pi - 1) = 2\pi \; \checkmark \]

This identity explains why both the proton (6) and strange baryons (7) derive from the same expression with different quantizations:

  • Proton: \(\lfloor 2\pi \rfloor = 6\)
  • Strange: \(\lceil 2\pi \rceil = 7\)

The q-Derivative

The q-derivative is defined as:

\[ D_q[f(x)] = \frac{f(qx) - f(x)}{(q-1)x} \]

For monomials:

\[ D_q[x^n] = [n]_q \cdot x^{n-1} \]

With \(q = \pi\):

\[ D_\pi[x^3] = [3]_\pi \cdot x^2 = 14.01 \cdot x^2 \]

The charm coefficient emerges directly from π-differentiation!

The charmed Lambda mass is literally \(m_e \times \pi^5 \times [3]_\pi\).

The \(e^\pi = 23\) Connection

A Stunning Result

\[ e^\pi = 23.14 \approx 23 = \text{double charm coefficient!} \]

The double charm baryon \(\Xi_{cc}^{++}\) has coefficient 23, which equals \(e^\pi\) to 0.6%!

This can also be derived from q-integers:

\[ 2[3]_\pi - \lceil [2]_\pi \rceil = 2 \times 14.01 - 5 = 23.02 \]

The "missing 5" when doubling charm is exactly \(\lceil [2]_\pi \rceil\) — the same coefficient that appears in bottom baryons!
