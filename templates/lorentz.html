Origin: Discrete-Continuous Duality

The correction arises from two different ways of summing a dimensional series \(f_n = (8\pi/n!) r^n\):

Discrete Sum (Quantum)

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{8\pi}{n!} \pi^n = 8\pi \cdot e^{\pi} = 581.59 \]

Particles exist at integer dimensions \(n = 0, 1, 2, ...\)

Continuous Integral (Classical)

\[ \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} 8\pi \cdot e^r \, dr = 16\pi \sinh(\pi) = 580.50 \]

Smooth distribution over all \(r \in [-\pi, \pi]\)

The Difference

\[ 8\pi e^{\pi} - 16\pi \sinh(\pi) = 8\pi e^{-\pi} \]

The "antimatter shadow" contribution is exactly \(8\pi e^{-\pi}\).

Why \(e^{-\pi}\)?

The key identity:

\[ e^{\pi} - 2\sinh(\pi) = e^{-\pi} \]

Proof:

\[ 2\sinh(\pi) = e^{\pi} - e^{-\pi} \] \[ e^{\pi} - 2\sinh(\pi) = e^{\pi} - (e^{\pi} - e^{-\pi}) = e^{-\pi} \]

Physical interpretation:

  • The discrete sum "sees" both \(e^{\pi}\) (matter) and \(e^{-\pi}\) (antimatter)
  • The continuous integral captures only \(2\sinh(\pi)\) (the net matter content)
  • The correction \(e^{-\pi}\) is what's "left behind" in the discrete-to-continuous transition
